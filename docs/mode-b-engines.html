NitroTron3 — Stutter Engine vs Grain Engine

1. Overview

Mode B has two distinct audio engines, selected by the K2 knob position:

Stutter Engine

Active when K2 is fully CCW (below 2% travel). A lightweight, probability-driven sample manipulator. Captures a small window of live input and randomly replays slices of it — creating micro-glitches, rhythmic chops, and momentary freezes.

Buffer: 200 ms • 2 voices • No pitch shift • No feedback

Grain Engine

Active when K2 is above 2%. A polyphonic granular synthesizer that scatters pitch-shifted fragments of the input across time. Creates shimmering textures, harmonic clouds, and evolving soundscapes above the dry bass.

Buffer: 8 seconds • 8 voices • Pitch-shifted • Feedback loop

The transition is instantaneous — turning K2 past the threshold switches engines with no crossfade or gap.

How It Works

A small circular buffer (9600 samples = 200 ms at 48 kHz) continuously captures the live input. Every sample, a random dice roll decides whether to trigger a stutter event. When triggered, the engine freezes a slice of the buffer and loops it back.

K3 is the sole control — it simultaneously scales probability, chunk size, repetitions, reverse chance, and cut-out chance. Fully CCW = clean passthrough, fully CW = dense chaotic stuttering.

Capture buffer: input is continuously written; events freeze and replay a slice

Event Types

Repeat Event

Captures a chunk (20–200 ms) from the buffer and loops it 1–5 times. Each event randomizes the chunk length (±40%), and may play in reverse (0–80% chance based on K3). Uses two alternating voices with Tukey-windowed crossfades for seamless looping.

Cut-Out Event

A momentary silence — the signal ducks to zero over a cosine envelope (10–50 ms). Creates rhythmic gaps in the signal. Cut-out probability is 0–40% of all events based on K3. No buffer playback involved — just smooth volume ducking.

Repeat event: two-voice crossfade with Tukey windows
Cut-out event: cosine ducking envelope

Two-Voice Crossfade

The stutter engine uses two alternating voices (ping-pong) to eliminate clicks at loop boundaries. When one voice reaches its tail taper, the other voice begins playing the next repetition with its own fade-in. The combined window sum is clamped to 1.0, ensuring a smooth handoff:

w = clamp(voice0.window + voice1.window, 0, 1)
wet = dry × (1 − w) + voice0_sample + voice1_sample

The dry signal fills any gap where neither voice is active. During the crossfade region, both voices overlap briefly, eliminating the discontinuity that would occur with a single-voice hard loop.
Two-voice ping-pong: Voice A ends as Voice B begins, windows overlap at loop boundary

How It Works

An 8-second ring buffer in SDRAM continuously records the input (plus feedback). A scheduler fires grain events at regular intervals with jitter. Each grain reads a fragment from somewhere in the buffer, pitch-shifts it, envelopes it with a Tukey window, and plays it back. Up to 8 grains can overlap simultaneously.

Ring buffer (8 s): grains read from different positions behind the write head

Scheduling & Overlap

The scheduler controls how often new grains fire and how many overlap. K3 drives both:

K3 low (smooth)

Long grains (200 ms), 4× overlap, minimal jitter, low reverse/loop chance. Produces a smooth, shimmering texture with gentle grain density. Grains overlap substantially, creating a continuous pad-like sound.

K3 high (chaotic)

Short grains (20 ms), 1× overlap, heavy jitter, high reverse/loop chance. Produces percussive, scattered fragments with rhythmic randomness. Grains barely overlap, creating a pointillist texture.

K3 low: long grains, high overlap, smooth texture
K3 high: short grains, sparse, percussive

Pitch & Harmony

Each grain gets a pitch-shift ratio computed from the live-tracked bass note plus the harmony mode (SW2):

SW2 PositionModeCharacter
UPFixed intervalK1 sets semitones (−24 to +24). Every grain at the same interval. Predictable parallel voice.
MIDDLEScale-quantized randomEach grain picks a random interval from a pentatonic scale, weighted toward K1. Melodic improvisation.
DOWNHarmonic cloudEach grain picks from the harmonic series. Creates chords and pads that follow the bass.

Feedback Loop

Post-HPF wet output, scaled by K5, is written back into the ring buffer. This means the grain engine hears itself — grains of grains of grains. At low K5, it adds subtle depth. At high K5, the loop self-sustains as a drone that evolves over the full 8-second buffer length.

Why feedback writes to the buffer, not the output: Traditional delay feedback stacks identical repeats. Writing to the buffer means each repetition gets re-scattered, re-pitched, and re-windowed by the grain engine — it evolves organically instead of decaying as an echo.

5. Window Functions

Both engines use the Tukey window (cosine-tapered rectangle) to eliminate clicks at grain boundaries. The key parameter is α — the fraction of the window spent in the cosine taper:

Tukey window shapes: α controls how much of the grain is tapered

Stutter: Fixed Taper

Taper length is computed as 10% of the slice length, clamped to 96–240 samples (2–5 ms). This gives just enough fade to avoid clicks while keeping the stutter punchy. The flat middle region dominates.

Grain: Adaptive Alpha

Alpha scales with grain length: short grains (20 ms) get α = 0.2 (mostly flat, percussive), long grains (200 ms) get α = 1.0 (full Hann, smooth). This means short grains punch through while long grains blend.
